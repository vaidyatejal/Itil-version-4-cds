Create, Deliver and Support (CDS)

Creative Mode

1. Organization and Culture

ITIL 4 CDS focuses heavily on the human, social, and cultural elements required to create and support services. The best tools fail without a structured, healthy team culture.

1.1 Organizational Structures

CDS identifies four basic structures. You must understand their direct operational trade-offs:

  • Functional Structure: Grouped strictly by technical specialties (e.g., separate teams for DBAs, Systems, Network, Developers).
    Pros: Extreme depth of expertise, clear career progression within the specialty.
    Cons: High communication silos, lack of empathy for customer experience, slow resolution hand-offs.
  • Divisional Structure: Grouped by business lines, geographical locations, or products (e.g., Team Retail, Team Commercial Banking).
    Pros: Heavy customer focus, rapid customized delivery.
    Cons: Inefficient duplication of roles and tools, fragmented technical standards across divisions.
  • Matrix Structure: Staff report to both a functional manager (for guild training) and project/product managers (for day-to-day work).
    Pros: Extremely flexible resource utilization.
    Cons: Double-reporting creates major friction and conflicting priorities for employees.
  • Flat Structure: Minimal or no middle management levels. Promotes fast, autonomous decision-making.
    Pros: High employee ownership, rapid responses.
    Cons: Extremely difficult to scale as the organization grows.
Real-World Analogy: Building a House

Imagine construction models:
• Functional: You hire one separate plumbing firm, one electrical firm, and one carpentry firm. They do their work independently. The quality is stellar, but because they don't coordinate, the plumber punches holes in the carpenter's drywall, leading to huge delays and finger-pointing.
• Divisional: You build a custom residential suburb and assign full, standalone teams to each home. House A team has its own plumber and electrician; House B has its own too. It's fast, but you pay double because you had to buy separate heavy machinery and tools for every single house.

1.2 Integrated and Collaborative Teams

To establish flow and break silos, organizations must build:

  • T-Shaped Skills: Professionals who have deep technical expertise in one specific area (the vertical bar of the 'T') combined with broad lateral working knowledge of adjacent technologies and general business priorities (the horizontal bar). This allows team members to step outside their direct role and support colleagues during bottlenecks.
  • Cross-Functional Teams: Multi-disciplinary groups that hold all skills needed to build, release, and support a product end-to-end, drastically reducing cross-team handovers.
Real-World Analogy: The Emergency Room (ER)

An ER nurse is a classic T-shaped professional. Their deep specialty is emergency nursing (triage, critical care). However, they also possess broad lateral knowledge of simple pharmacy, lab operations, radiology, and customer relations. If the ER becomes overwhelmed with trauma cases, they can swarm and assist doctors or radiologists immediately, rather than waiting in an isolated silo.

Psychological Safety & Servant Leadership

Coined by Amy Edmondson, **Psychological Safety** is a shared cultural belief that a team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking (e.g., admitting mistakes, raising concerns, asking questions, or suggesting new ideas without fear of punishment, ridicule, or career damage).

CDS highlights that psychological safety is absolutely mandatory to sustain a continuous improvement culture. To foster this, managers must practice **Servant Leadership**—focusing on supporting the team, clearing operational blockers, securing resources, and delegating authority, rather than executing traditional command-and-control.

1.3 Outcome-Based Metrics & Watermelons

ITIL 4 CDS urges organizations to shift their measuring focus away from mere outputs to actual outcomes:

  • Outputs: What the team does or builds (e.g., "We closed 250 tickets", "We deployed 5 software updates"). Outputs are easy to measure but do not indicate real value.
  • Outcomes: The business results realized by the customer (e.g., "Online checkout downtime was reduced, allowing 12% more sales transactions").
Critical Exam Concept: Watermelon Metrics

A **Watermelon Metric** describes a service level indicator that appears **Green on the outside** (all technical SLA metrics are fully met, such as 99.9% server availability or 15-minute support response times) but is actually **Red on the inside** (users are extremely unhappy because the core application is slow, frustrating to navigate, or the overall user experience is terrible).

Real-World Analogy: A restaurant guarantees that your food will be delivered to your table in under 10 minutes (SLA). The waiter delivers your food in 8 minutes, so the dashboard is "green". However, the food is completely cold and burnt. The metric is green, but the outcome is a failure because the customer is angry.

2. Information and Technology

Information and technology are the lifeblood of modern service management. CDS focuses on tools that increase visibility, reduce routine human labor, and predict issues.

2.1 Integrated Toolchains & Advanced Analytics

Traditional IT departments operated with isolated systems (e.g., the service desk tool didn't communicate with the developer's bug tracker). CDS mandates an **integrated toolchain**. Continuous data sharing across deployment pipelines, test platforms, configuration databases (CMDB), and the service desk provides rich, end-to-end data pipelines. This allows managers to run advanced predictive analytics, spotting performance bottlenecks before they turn into major outages.

2.2 RPA, AI, Machine Learning, and AIOps

Modern technology transforms how operational support is delivered:

  • Robotic Process Automation (RPA): Software agents programmed to execute high-volume, repetitive, rules-based tasks without human intervention.
    Typical CDS cases: Resetting passwords, automated server log checking, standard Active Directory provisioning.
  • AI & Machine Learning (ML): Systems that recognize complex patterns and make intelligent assessments.
    Typical CDS cases: Natural language processing (NLP) chatbots triage tier-1 user support; ML automatically correlates incident history to route new tickets to the right team.
  • AIOps (Artificial Intelligence for IT Operations): Applying machine learning directly to system logs and telemetry.
    Typical CDS cases: Correlating thousands of simultaneous background system alerts, filtering out trivial noise, and identifying real system anomalies to prevent massive service disruptions.
Real-World Analogy: The Soda Factory

Let's map these tools to a beverage production line:
• RPA: An automated mechanical arm that picks up a cap and screws it onto a bottle. It's incredibly fast at doing that one exact repetitive task. But if a bottle arrives slightly dented or misaligned, the RPA bot jams because it has no "brain" to adjust (rules-based only).
• AI/ML: A smart camera that takes a picture of each passing bottle, compares it to thousands of images, and instantly decides if the bottle is "clean", "scratched", or "faulty" (pattern recognition).
• AIOps: A central smart control system monitoring the factory's background sounds, vibrations, temperature, and electrical draw. It correlates a microscopic drop in air pressure in Room 3 with a slight squeak in Conveyor 2, predicting that a motor will fail in 3 hours, and automatically dispatches a repair request.

2.3 CI/CD Pipelines

Continuous Integration (CI) automatically compiles and runs automated unit tests whenever developers commit code changes. Continuous Delivery (CD) automates the deployment of tested code into production or staging environments, ensuring releases are fast, frequent, and safe.

Real-World Analogy: Newspaper Printing

Instead of writing all articles and printing a massive book once a year (traditional waterfall release), journalists write articles, automated digital fact-checkers and editors check the grammar and verify facts instantly (Continuous Integration), and the articles are continuously streamed to online readers every hour (Continuous Delivery).

3. Service Value Streams

A **Value Stream** is a series of specific, sequential steps an organization undertakes to co-create value with consumers. The **Service Value Chain (SVC)** is the general operating model consisting of 6 core activities (Plan, Improve, Engage, Design & Transition, Obtain/Build, Deliver & Support).

Service Value Chain vs. Value Streams

Real-World Analogy: The **Service Value Chain** is like a fully-equipped professional kitchen. It contains all the tools, appliances, and general capabilities (stoves, ovens, refrigerators, blenders, chefs).

A **Value Stream** is a specific **recipe** used to prepare a dish. To make a chocolate cake (deliver a new service), you combine the kitchen capabilities in a precise sequence: get ingredients from the fridge (Obtain/Build), mix them (Design), bake in the oven (Transition), and serve to the customer (Deliver & Support).

3.1 Value Stream for a New Service (Step-by-Step)

To design, develop, and transition a brand new service from demand to live value, an organization walks through the value chain:

  1. Engage: Discuss requirements directly with stakeholders, understand user pain points, and define the service vision.
  2. Plan: Establish the project budget, assess the business case, and allocate core resources.
  3. Design & Transition: Architect the service components, design the user interfaces, and outline target service level targets.
  4. Obtain / Build: Write the application code, integrate third-party APIs, or configure database instances.
  5. Design & Transition (again): Test the build in staging, validate security compliance, and deploy code to production.
  6. Deliver & Support: Expose the new features to users (Release), establish ongoing operational monitoring, and provide the service desk with user training manuals.

3.2 Value Stream for User Support (Incident Management)

When a customer encounters a technical error or outage, restoring service follows a specific flow:

  1. Engage: The user reports the outage via phone or self-service portal. The Service Desk registers and prioritizes the ticket.
  2. Deliver & Support: Operational support agents check the Knowledge base for documented workarounds. If a simple fix exists, they apply it immediately.
  3. Obtain / Build (If needed): If the issue requires a technical hotfix or physical replacement component, a developer builds the patch or the procurement team sources the hardware.
  4. Design & Transition: The technical patch is deployed or the hardware is configured to match organizational security standards.
  5. Deliver & Support: The service is restored, the support agent verifies application access with the user, and closes the ticket.
  6. Improve / Engage: Gather user satisfaction feedback, and update knowledge records to facilitate rapid future resolutions.

4. Core ITIL Practices in CDS

Practices are integrated sets of organizational resources (people, processes, technology, partners) designed to perform specific work. You must know how they contribute across value streams.

Crucial Exam Distinction: Deployment vs. Release

In ITIL 4, **Deployment** and **Release** are completely distinct.
• **Deployment Management** is technical (e.g., copying compiled code to production servers at 2:00 AM on Sunday). The code is there, but users cannot see it because it's behind a feature toggle.
• **Release Management** is business-centric (e.g., turning on the feature flag at 9:00 AM on Tuesday and launching marketing campaigns).

Software can be deployed to production weeks before it is officially released to customers.

5. Prioritization and Work Management

IT departments are constantly flooded with work (incidents, feature requests, system upgrades). CDS teaches strategies to manage backlogs and accelerate flow.

5.1 Work in Progress (WIP) Limits

Unmanaged work leads to massive queues. When developers or support agents multitask across dozens of open items, "context switching" wastes time and stalls flow. CDS advocates **WIP Limits** (derived from Kanban): restricting the number of active tasks a team can work on simultaneously. By forcing the team to *finish* existing tasks before *starting* new ones, delivery speeds increase dramatically.

5.2 Swarming Support Models

Traditional IT support uses hierarchical tiers: Tier 1 (Service Desk) fails -> Tier 2 (Desktop Support) fails -> Tier 3 (Vendors/Developers). This sequence creates massive waiting queues.
CDS advocates **Swarming**—a collaborative support model where cross-functional experts assemble to solve incidents as a single group. There are three primary swarms:

  • Dispatch Swarm: Meets briefly and frequently to rapidly triage incoming incidents, resolving simple ones instantly and sending complex ones to the best swarms.
  • Backlog Swarm: Gathers regularly to tackle stubborn, long-standing, unresolved incidents by bringing together diverse experts (e.g., DBA, network admin, developer) to brainstorm.
  • Drop-in Swarm: Formed immediately when a major, high-priority outage strikes (e.g., checkout system crashes).
Real-World Analogy: Swarming in Action

• Dispatch Swarm: Like ER doctors standing at the ambulance bay. They quickly look at incoming patients, patch minor cuts immediately, and send critical cases directly to surgery.
• Backlog Swarm: A weekly hospital board meeting where various specialists (cardiology, neurology, oncology) review a patient with highly complex, undiagnosed symptoms together.
• Drop-in Swarm: A "Code Blue" emergency. The alarm rings, and the anesthesiologist, cardiologist, and trauma nurses immediately run to the patient's room to resuscitate them together.

5.3 Prioritization Models

  • MoSCoW: Categorizes demands into **M**ust have, **S**hould have, **C**ould have, and **W**on't have. Useful for rapid scope management.
  • Weighted Shortest Job First (WSJF): A mathematical scheduling model. It calculates the **Cost of Delay** (business urgency + risk reduction + value lost by waiting) and divides it by the **Job Size/Duration**. Jobs that yield massive value and require little execution time are scheduled first (quick wins).

5.4 The "Shift-Left" Strategy

**Shift-Left** means moving tasks, testing, or issue resolution closer to the source of demand or earlier in the service lifecycle.

Real-World Analogy: Hotel Room Safes

Traditionally, if a hotel guest forgets their in-room safe combination, they must call the front desk, who dispatches the hotel security manager (Tier-2 support) with a physical master key. This takes 30 minutes, leading to guest frustration.

The hotel decides to **Shift-Left**: they place a clear, simple 3-step diagram on the safe door and program a "reset passcode via SMS" option. Now, the guest resolves their own issue in 30 seconds. The resolution has shifted "left" directly to the customer, freeing up the security manager for real safety tasks.

6. Sourcing & SIAM

In modern enterprise IT, companies rarely build or operate every component in-house. Strategic vendor management is essential.

6.1 Sourcing Models

  • Insourcing: Keep all capabilities inside, relying on direct internal staff.
  • Outsourcing: Contract out complete operational responsibility of a service to an external vendor.
  • Co-Sourcing: Collaborative model where internal teams work hand-in-hand with external supplier resources to deliver a service.

6.2 Service Integration and Management (SIAM)

When an enterprise uses multiple diverse external IT suppliers (e.g., AWS for cloud, Salesforce for CRM, a local MSP for desks, a telecom firm for WAN), managing them individually leads to massive integration gaps, commercial finger-pointing, and disjointed service delivery during outages.

**SIAM** introduces a dedicated **Service Integrator** layer. The Service Integrator sits between the client business and the multiple suppliers. The Integrator does not necessarily deliver technical work; instead, it coordinates all suppliers under a single set of operating standards, unified end-to-end SLAs, and shared incident handling workflows.

Real-World Analogy: The Wedding Planner

A couple planning a large wedding (the client business) has to hire a DJ, a caterer, a florist, a photographer, and a venue manager (the multiple suppliers). If the couple coordinates them individually on their wedding day, it's a disaster—the DJ plays music too early, the caterer serves food late, and they blame each other.

The couple hires a **Wedding Planner (Service Integrator)**. The planner coordinates all suppliers, ensuring the DJ plays the announcement music at the exact second the caterer brings out the cake. The planner integrates the separate services into a seamless experience.
